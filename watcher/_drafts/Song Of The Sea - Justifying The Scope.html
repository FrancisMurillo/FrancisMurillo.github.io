Song Of The Sea

Song of the Sea uses mirroring to justify the scope of the film.

Song of the Sea (2014) is one the best hand drawn film I have ever seen that belongs on the list of the greatest. Like with Shinkai films, every frame in the movie can be considered a wallpaper and it holds up to this day. I rarely notice or comment on the music but it feels so mystical and unique that it pales in comparison with the standard music affair of sounding the same, avoiding copyright and so on. I could probably sing praises all day but I am here to talk about why Saoirse song matters.

SPOILER ALERT

The story is about a boy accepting the loss of her mother and saving her sister so why did her song save the whole of the spirit world? At the face, all her song needed to do is cure her and implicitly call her mother to turn her into a human. Was there really a need to free the spirits? Let us think about the meaning film and see where it takes us.

My reasoning is within the film itself, I am not an expert in myth so I can only go so far. Just a reminder.

Saoirse's Song

In answering why her song was global in saving the world, let's consider its meaning. According to the film with an early scene by Ben which I found at the script: 

BEN: She's singing her song so that she can send all the fairies home
across the sea.

There is also a reminder of this with the musical Deenashee after they sing the nice jig or song: 

DEENASHEE: Human child, find the selkie's true coat, then she can sing
the song and save us all.

The scene with the Great Senachai also hints at this: 

GREAT SENACHIA: She'll be turned to stone soon, no doubt. You see,
without her coat, the selkie has no voice. And without her song, she'll
not last the night. And we'll all share in her fate.

So with these three dialogue is it still not foreshadowed correctly? I think it is still not that well foreshadowed since it is not shown or elaborated that the song would carry such scope. The word you might use here is "hinted", a good way for the song's scope be established is that Bronach sings and shows the spirit being led home.

However, does it matter so much whether her song saves all or just herself. At face value, it ties everyone to a resolved ending. Also her singing is known to be magical, her stifled singing through the shell's voice was enough to break Macha's magic. Once she had a real voice, the world listened. So despite being only hinted, it shouldn't be a problem in terms of mechanics?

I think the problem most people have is that it seems like it did come out of nowhere. From a casual viewing, it might be that way. Only thinking about it would you really understand and appreciate the use of symbols and parallels. By exploring at the tricks it employs, her song might really have more to it than just a pretty and cute voice.

Bronach's Song

If I called Bronach singing would be a foreshadowing, why didn't she sing before Saoirse and Ben were born before dying in childbirth? To reason out for her, it might be as simple as she can't not that she won't. Before she died, did she lose her magic? We have no direct evidence of this but it is established that the story of Mac Lir and Macha is known means that she wasn't able to use her magic to free Mac Lir prior to the legends Also it is quite a question whether being with the father stripped her of her powers as in Ponyo or The Little Mermaid. She might also have used last her of powers simply to give birth to her Selkie daughter.

Bottom line, she can't and the story has no need to delve into this aspect of her magic. It does setup or leave it to her daughter to carry out her lost duty whatever the prior reason might have been.

One of the symbolic moments for Bronach's legacy is where Ben teaches Saoirse the song his mother taught her but not her sister. If she did not teach this song, would there be a song to activate her coat and save her in the first place? It is also quite curious that Saoirse does not know the song being six years old meaning Ben is withdrawing the love and legacy of her mother which really adds more to what she sings. 

BRONACH: My son. Remember me, in your stories and in your songs. Know
that I will always love you. Always.

That beautiful dialogue holds a lot of meaning but for now it emphasizes Ben forgiving Saoirse by giving her the song. In doing so, she carries the legacy of her mother meaning that there father can now be awaken by the fact her wife's love carries on there child as indicated by this dialogue: 

CONOR: Bronach. I loved you so much. I still do.

CONOR: It's as though I've been asleep, all these years. I'm so sorry.

The choice of Saoirse staying is also monumental, by staying she acknowledges and forgives Ben thus completing his arc and their father. The problem of the family has been resolved by the passing of this song and thus saving their family which is the equivalent of saving their own little world.

But again this does not answer why the song is that global, all that is needed for this song is to call there mother from the other side and give Saoirse the choice of staying. So where does the resolution of spirit world fit in here?

Duality

One of the tricks this anime uses is mirroring the characters. What I want you to consider is how they are similar in design and story. It is akin to having a human and spiritual counterpart.

Granny and Macha
Mothers who want to comfort there mourning sons
Conor and Mac Lir
Sons who have lost there wives
Ferryman Dan and Great Senachia
Guides to there respective world
Saoirse and Bronach
Bridges to there world
Cu and Mac Lir Dogs
Dogs

There are more similarities such as there voice actors which is probably not an oversight but these are the core ones. So what does having mirrors in there respective world actually do? They actually make the spirit and physical world intertwined or interchangeable. 

BRONACH: All of my kind must leave tonight. But, Saoirse, you are part
human. If I take her coat, our worlds will be untangled.

Untangled. That fact that there is no magical portal that takes the protagonist to a new world emphasizes this. Each of the places where the story proceeds is within one world, be it a sewer or a forest or there home. This is enhanced by the fact that the people that Ben know are also the same people in the other world. If the parallels are correct, helping the spirit world means helping the physical world. The solution lies in the other side.

This is not Wizard of Oz (1939) where Dorothy wakes up and finds the people he knew on the dream world in the real world. There parallels did not exist before but after the story making it just a happy coincidence, where as in this film it pushes the idea that there is no separation between the worlds. But in this film this is symbolic again, how does the song fit in saving the spirits?

Family Story

So if you took out the part where the spirits are returning home and just have Saoirse sing for five minutes, you would have a blank scene without meaning. (If you think about it, it really might just be meaningful filler to tie everything nicely.) As I mentioned above the Selkie is supposed to lead the spirit back home so a montage of spirits isn't really far off. What I want to posit is that by mirroring the physical and mystical world, the feelings of Ben with Saoirse is translated to the world.

According to the Senachai, if Ben fails to complete his journey, the spirits may fall into a stony fate. If what he says is true, it means there might not be any magic that can counter Macha or that there are no other Selkie that are alive except Saoirse to do so. I am assuming that she is the only living Selkie around. So the world is at stake.

The journey of Ben is the hero story, traveling to the underworld and coming to grips with his maternal grief. Would simply getting Saoirse the coat be the only objective without bonding with her? The three spirits that Ben meet emphasize how important the song is to saving the spirits, saving her and forgiving her. If any skimped out, would Ben understand and tell her the song? Without those, would Saoirse stay given the choice? By the meaning of her choice, she wouldn't stay and deny Ben the forgiveness and freedom he deserves. Forgetting about the journey, is the spirit world still saved regardless? You might say they are just as long she sings.

I believe that Saoirse wouldn't sing even if he told her without actually forgiving her but it is indeed only my belief of the symbolic meaning of the act not the logistic of it. So am I saying the family problem is within the family? Yes and no. It is indeed just a family problem but by mirroring the character and the world, everyone else is tied to it.

World Story

When Macha's emotion restored, she said: 

MACHA: I have been so lost for so long.
BEN: Macha?
MACHA: Please, forgive me. I couldn't bear to see my son suffering so much. I couldn't
bear it.

After Mac Lir and Macha go to the light, we see Granny meet up with her son and says: 

CONOR: We're all right now, Mum. We're all right now.

In perspective, Macha's redemption was only fulfilled by when Mac Lir was awakened by Saoirse. Moments later, we have Granny's son apologizing to her mother after relaying awaking from his own slumber. To reiterate the words he used after meeting her wife: 

CONOR: It's as though I've been asleep,

There is symmetry and flow. The mirroring of the characters make it so that the fate of the family is tied with them. By helping the selkie Saoirse, she wakes up Mac Lir and Macha move on, thus preventing further stoning or sleeping of the spirit. Paralleling, Saoirse finds her voice, she wakes up her father by calling there mother which resolves Granny's problem. So whether the song should be global or not, it does tie everyone akin to where destroying the villain releases the curse. Convenience in logistics you can say. What I find beautiful about the mirroring here is that you can see indirectly how the resolution reflects each other which is not a coincidence.

The journey of Ben is the plight of the world. This is really subtle and one might use the world-type story archetype or the Sekaikei but that might be pushing it. For me, the scene of Saoirse singing is quite cathartic even if it isn't obvious at first.

Conclusion

If Macha was just a generic witch who hates the world and Mac Lir didn't exist, I might be calling out that Saoirse is a savior; but there is so much visual and symbolic subtlety and depth that I can't help but see more in the song and the salvation. The story is simple at face value where kids and adults can understand like with Inside Out (2015) but also find depth in it. This is why this film is one of my favorite animated film: beautiful and deep as the sea.
